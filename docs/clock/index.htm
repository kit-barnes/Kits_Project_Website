DIGITAL CLOCK

This clock is the oldest of the projects I've documented here. Regrettably, I cannot say exactly how old. Best guess is I designed and built it in 1972 plus or minus a year or two. At the time digital clocks were a novelty after centuries of round clock faces with hands and small red LED 7-segment displays were starting to appear everywhere.

My design was inspired by and designed around the newly available SLA-13H: a large GREEN display. The use of a clock IC was obvious. It allowed the circuit to fit into something smaller than a breadbox, even if I did have to forgo the pleasure, pain, and satisfaction of designing it myself. Wanting a wooden case, I picked a small arts and crafts box, turned it upside down, put rubber feet on it and worked hard to fit the circuit inside. (Note the resistors standing on end.) The MINIMAL venting (slots on top and round holes on the bottom) was added after I realized just how hot it was going to get in that wooden box. It's proving adequate though - Coming on 50 years and nothing's burned out. (With the exception of Q2 which was replaced, probably in the 90's.)

Last winter the transformer hum seemed to worsen and I opened the case (for the first time in many years), took the photos here and measured the transformer. I ordered a replacement but have not yet been inspired (or sufficiently annoyed) to install it.

In the first photo you can see part of the printed wiring on the display board. "Printed" may be a bit of a misnomer - "drawn" is more correct. Since my personal projects were always "one-offs", I happily abandoned photoresist as soon as I discovered etch resist pens. I would first drill the holes (using a piece of marked perf-board for a pattern) then draw the resist onto the board with the pen. The technique worked well for double-sided boards too, allowing that none of the holes were plated through.

Documentation for the clock is 8 sheets of paper, ignored in my files for years. When I took it out to digitize it for this page I was suprised to realize these were not the originals but Xerox copies. I vaguely remember making the copies because the originals were in bad shape, but I have no idea what happened to the originals. At least one sheet is missing. There should be a drawing showing the printed wiring for the main board.
